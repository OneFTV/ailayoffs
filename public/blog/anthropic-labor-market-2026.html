Anthropic's New AI Labor Market Study: Who's Really at Risk?

📅 March 5, 2026 📖 5 min read 🏷️ Research

TL;DR: Anthropic published new research introducing "observed exposure" — a metric that measures not just what AI could do, but what it's actually doing. Computer programmers are #1 at 75% task coverage. No mass unemployment yet, but hiring of young workers (22-25) is quietly slowing in exposed jobs.

A New Way to Measure AI Risk

Previous studies measured AI's theoretical capability — what tasks could an LLM theoretically speed up? Anthropic's new paper flips the script by combining theory with real-world usage data from millions of Claude conversations.

The result is a metric called "observed exposure" that weights:

  • Tasks that are theoretically feasible for AI
  • Tasks that see actual usage in professional settings
  • Work-related contexts (not personal use)
  • Automated implementations weighted more heavily than augmentative ones

The key insight: AI is far from reaching its theoretical capability. Even in the most exposed category (Computer & Math), Claude currently covers just 33% of all possible tasks.

Least Exposed — The "AI-Proof" Jobs

30% of all workers have zero AI exposure. These jobs involve physical, hands-on tasks that AI simply can't reach:

  • Cooks, Bartenders, Dishwashers
  • Motorcycle Mechanics
  • Lifeguards
  • Dressing Room Attendants

Many tasks remain beyond AI's reach — from pruning trees and operating farm machinery to representing clients in court.

The Uncomfortable Finding: Young Workers

While the study found no systematic increase in unemployment for highly exposed workers overall, one finding stands out:

"We find suggestive evidence that hiring of younger workers has slowed in exposed occupations... a 14% drop in the job finding rate compared to 2022 in exposed occupations."

Workers aged 22-25 are less likely to be hired into AI-exposed occupations since ChatGPT launched. The effect isn't huge, but it echoes findings from other researchers (Brynjolfsson et al.). Young people aren't being fired — they're just not being hired in the first place.

Who's Most Vulnerable?

The demographics of the most exposed workers may surprise you:

  • 16% more likely to be female than unexposed workers
  • 11% more likely to be white
  • Almost 2x more likely to be Asian
  • Earn 47% more on average
  • 3.8x more likely to hold a graduate degree

This isn't a blue-collar displacement story. AI is coming for educated, well-paid, white-collar workers first.

BLS Projections Align

The Bureau of Labor Statistics independently projects weaker job growth for occupations with higher AI exposure. For every 10 percentage point increase in observed exposure, the BLS growth projection drops by 0.6 percentage points through 2034.

The paper notes this is a modest correlation — but it validates the measure against independent government data.

Why This Matters

Previous studies told us what AI could replace. This study tells us what AI is actually replacing — right now, based on real usage data. The gap between theoretical capability and actual adoption is massive, but it's closing fast.

As the authors note: "This framework is most useful when the effects are ambiguous — and could help identify the most vulnerable jobs before displacement is visible."
